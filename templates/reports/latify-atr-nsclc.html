The structural argument

LATIFY tested whether adding ATR inhibition to anti-PD-L1 therapy could rescue response in NSCLC patients who had already progressed on anti-PD-(L)1 in the first-line setting. The structural problem with this design is that the tumour population enrolled had already demonstrated escape from the immune-surveillance step that PD-(L)1 blockade addresses. The question was not whether ATR + PD-L1 could work in an ICI-naive tumour, but whether it could re-engage an immune step that had already failed.

ATR inhibition addresses DNA damage response, a different layer of biology from the effector encounter step that PD-(L)1 blockade covers. In a tumour where the immune encounter step has already been evaded, adding a DNA-damage-response modulator does not restore the missing structural element. The combination changes the damage signal but not the immune-cycle step that the tumour has escaped from.

The framework places LATIFY in the same structural class as other trials that have tried to re-engage a failed immune step by adding a different mechanism alongside it, rather than by covering a new step. That class has a poor track record in post-ICI progression settings, and LATIFY was not expected to be the exception.
