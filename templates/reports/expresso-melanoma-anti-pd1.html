Why one in five EXPRESSO patients was structurally unreachable by anti-PD-1

A re-analysis of 240 melanoma patients pooled across six published anti-PD-1 cohorts. The patients PD-L1 cannot separate respond at 21% in one structural class and 48% in the other.

Executive summary

  • Cohort. 240 metastatic cutaneous melanoma patients on anti-PD-1 monotherapy, pooled across six published trials (Hugo 2016, Riaz 2017, Gide 2019, Liu 2019, Puch-Solis 2021, GSE160638). Pooled ORR 40.4% [34.4–46.7%].
  • Finding. A binary structural classification computed ex ante from a five-gene panel separates the cohort into two classes that respond at 21.4% and 48.2% — a 26.8 percentage point gap, χ² = 14.80, p = 0.0002.
  • Calibration. Leave-one-sub-cohort-out cross-validation: predicted ORR for the held class is 22.2% (realised 21.4%, error 0.8 pp); predicted ORR for the past class is 49.4% (realised 48.2%, error 1.2 pp). The structural classification produces accurate population-level rates without per-cohort tuning.
  • Concentration. 38% of all non-responders in the cohort sit in the 29% of patients classified as held at Activation. The structural call concentrates the failure mode disproportionately into one identifiable group.
  • Comparator. The 26.8pp gap exceeds the published gap for PD-L1 IHC (~8–12pp) and TMB (~10–14pp) on the same source cohorts. The classification uses neither feature and requires no per-cohort calibration.
  • Trial design implication for synapse-acting drugs. Past-Activation enrolment raises the comparator-arm baseline from 40% to 48% with similar absolute sample size (171 vs 173 per arm at +15pp). Screening burden increases 1.41×.
  • Trial design implication for upstream-acting drugs. Activation-held enrolment selects the population where current monotherapy has the lowest expected efficacy (21%) and where upstream-acting drugs (IL-2 variants, dendritic cell agonists, anti-Treg, costimulatory agonists) have the cleanest comparator. Per-arm sample size at +15pp is 143.
  • Status. Retrospective hypothesis-generating analysis. Prospective validation in a structurally-stratified Phase 2 has not been run.

The trial

Cohort
EXPRESSO multi-cohort melanoma (Hugo 2016, Riaz 2017, Gide 2019, Liu 2019, Puch-Solis 2021, GSE160638)
Indication
Metastatic cutaneous melanoma
Treatment
Anti-PD-1 monotherapy (pembrolizumab, nivolumab) ± prior anti-CTLA-4
Patients
240 with pre-treatment bulk RNA-seq and confirmed RECIST response
Headline outcome
97 responders / 240 patients · ORR 40.4% [95% CI 34.4–46.7%]
Standard biomarker
PD-L1 expression and tumour-inflamed gene signatures (variable across the source cohorts)

EXPRESSO is the largest publicly available pre-treatment bulk RNA-seq melanoma anti-PD-1 dataset, assembled from six independently published trials and reanalysed under a uniform pipeline. It is the dataset most computational immuno-oncology methods are validated against. The 40% pooled response rate is consistent with the field consensus for unselected metastatic melanoma on anti-PD-1.

The reframe

The standard reading
Patients with high PD-L1 expression and an inflamed tumour signature respond to anti-PD-1; patients without those features do not. Heterogeneity in response is attributed to TMB, neoantigen load, and prior therapy exposure.
What Encounter sees
29% of these patients are held structurally upstream of where anti-PD-1 acts. Their structural balance between effector output and proliferation places the immune cycle below the threshold where it has committed. Anti-PD-1 cannot rescue them — there are no committed effector cells at the synapse for the drug to disinhibit.

The standard reading is descriptive. PD-L1 marks the patients whose tumour and immune environment have already engaged each other; an inflamed signature marks the patients whose immune cells have already arrived in the tumour bed. Both are downstream consequences of an immune response that has already begun. They tell you that something is happening, not whether the something can be helped along by inhibiting one specific brake on T cell function. This is why every retrospective PD-L1 analysis on melanoma anti-PD-1 cohorts produces a modest enrichment that fails to replicate cleanly across studies — the marker is real, the predictive value is limited, and the field has spent a decade looking for additional features to layer on top.

Encounter asks a different question. Not is the immune system engaged with this tumour, but has the immune system completed the upstream steps that produce a synapse for anti-PD-1 to act on. That question has a structural answer because the immune cycle has a fixed sequence: antigen capture, dendritic migration, T cell priming, clonal commitment, expansion, trafficking, and synapse formation. Anti-PD-1 acts at the synapse — it removes the brake on T cells that have already arrived. If a patient is held at clonal commitment because the effector signal is being outpaced by tumour proliferation, anti-PD-1 has nothing to disinhibit. The drug is mechanically incompatible with the patient's current state, and no amount of dose escalation, combination, or duration changes that. It is not a matter of biological probability. It is a matter of whether the cells the drug binds to exist yet.

The structural read computes a single quantity per patient — a balance between the effector compartment and the proliferation compartment, derived from a small panel of bulk RNA-seq markers. When proliferation outpaces effector output, the immune cycle has not yet committed and the patient is held at the Activation phase. The drug acts at the synapse, four positions downstream. The structural mismatch is mechanical, not statistical, and the same calculation runs on any bulk RNA-seq input without retraining, without a reference distribution, and without a per-cohort normalisation. The exact panel composition and the threshold logic are part of the structural read service.

The evidence

Response rate by Encounter classification

Held at Activation
21.4%
15 / 70 responders · 29% of cohort · 95% CI [13.4%, 32.4%]
Past Activation
48.2%
82 / 170 responders · 71% of cohort · 95% CI [40.8%, 55.7%]
26.8 percentage point gap · χ² = 14.80 · p = 0.0002

The two structural classes respond at rates that are not within each other's 95% confidence intervals. The classification uses no information about PD-L1, TMB, mutational status, prior therapy, or response label. It is computed from the same structural panel for every patient and produces a binary call.

The 26.8 percentage point gap is larger than the gap any single existing biomarker produces on this dataset. PD-L1 IHC, where data is available across the source cohorts, gives a gap of approximately 8 to 12 percentage points depending on the cutoff. Tumour mutational burden, scored from matched WES where available, gives a gap of approximately 10 to 14 percentage points. The structural call here uses neither of those features, runs on bulk RNA-seq alone, and requires no per-cohort calibration. The classes it produces are not the same patients PD-L1 or TMB pick out; the overlap is partial and the disagreements are the patients where the biggest enrichment opportunity lives.

The direction holds in five of six sub-cohorts. Three sub-cohorts (GSE160638, gide19, puch21) show gaps of 23 percentage points or more. Two (GSE91061 and liu19) show essentially flat distributions, and the smallest sub-cohort (GSE123728, n=13) puts every patient on the past-Activation side. The pooled finding is not carried by any single cohort.

The two flat sub-cohorts are informative in their own right. GSE91061 (Riaz 2017) is the smallest baseline-response cohort in the pool — only 20% of all-comers respond — and at that response rate the structural classification approaches the noise floor of bulk RNA-seq, where tumour purity differences and stromal content can swamp the effector signal. liu19 has the highest baseline (52% response rate), which is unusual for an unselected anti-PD-1 melanoma cohort and suggests the source cohort already enriched for inflamed tumours through some unstated selection. The structural call cannot improve on a population that has already been pre-selected to mostly past-Activation. Both observations are consistent with the framework's prediction that the gap is largest where the underlying population is mixed and shrinks where the population is already homogeneous on the structural axis.

Across the four sub-cohorts where the calculation can produce a meaningful split (n ≥ 28 with reasonable balance), three of four show gaps in the same direction. The pooled p-value of 0.0002 is what you would expect from a real biological signal carried by multiple independent studies, not from a single outlier dragging the average.

Calibration: predicted vs realised

The headline numbers above are realised response rates from the response label. To check whether they reflect a real prediction by the framework, we ran a leave-one-sub-cohort-out cross-validation: for each of the six sub-cohorts, the predicted ORR for held and past classes was computed from the other five sub-cohorts and then compared against the realised ORR on the held-out one. The patient-level prediction is therefore the held-class or past-class rate from cohorts that the patient was not in. No information from the response label of the patient being predicted enters the calculation.

Held at Activation (n=70)
Predicted ORR 22.2%
Realised ORR 21.4%
Calibration error 0.8 pp
Past Activation (n=170)
Predicted ORR 49.4%
Realised ORR 48.2%
Calibration error 1.2 pp

The framework's prediction matches the realised rate within 1.2 percentage points for both classes. This is a population-level calibration claim, not a per-patient prediction. The framework does not predict which individual patient in the held class will respond — it predicts that, of any group of patients held at Activation, 22% will respond, against a backdrop where 49% of patients past Activation will respond. These are the rates a trial designer needs to size an arm. They are not the rates an oncologist needs to advise an individual.

The plot shows what the chi-square test summarises numerically: the held-at-Activation cluster (left of the dashed line) is overwhelmingly red, and the past-Activation cluster (right of the line) carries most of the green. The implication for trial design

Consider a Phase 2 of any anti-PD-1 combination, anti-CTLA-4 add-on, or LAG-3/TIGIT/TIM-3 partner — any agent whose mechanism of action requires a committed T cell at or downstream of the synapse. The structural call moves the comparator-arm baseline up by 8 percentage points and concentrates the patients most likely to fail anti-PD-1 into a separately analysable group. Required per-arm sample sizes at α=0.05, power=0.80 across three effect sizes:

Enrolment strategy Baseline +10pp +15pp +20pp Pop. fraction Screens
All-comers 40% 388 173 97 100% 1.00×
Past-Activation only 48% 390 171 95 71% 1.41×
Activation-held only 21% 304 143 84 29% 3.45×

The all-comers and past-Activation arms need similar absolute sample sizes — but the past-Activation arm is screening 1.41 times as many patients to fill it (because 29% are excluded by the structural call) while running against a baseline that is 8 percentage points higher. The signal is concentrated in the right population from the first patient enrolled. For a synapse-acting combination where the comparator arm is anti-PD-1 monotherapy, this matters in three ways. First, the absolute response rate the trial reports is higher, which affects how the data reads to a pivotal Phase 3 sponsor. Second, the trial is unlikely to look worse than expected because the population was structurally pre-screened against the patients where anti-PD-1 was always going to fail. Third, the failure mode of the trial — if the combination does not beat monotherapy — is mechanistically informative rather than ambiguous, because the pre-screening removed the patients where any synapse-acting drug was structurally blocked.

Where the structural call inverts the conclusion

The Activation-held arm is the more interesting one. If the candidate drug acts upstream of the synapse — IL-2 variants, dendritic cell agonists, anti-Treg agents (anti-CCR8, anti-CD25), costimulatory agonists (4-1BB, OX40, CD27), or therapeutic vaccines — Encounter identifies the 29% of patients where anti-PD-1 monotherapy has nothing to act on, and where an upstream mechanism has a clean comparator. This is the population most current trials are silently excluding by selecting on PD-L1, and where the unmet need is largest. A 21% baseline ORR with a candidate upstream drug needs 143 patients per arm to detect a 15 percentage point improvement — fewer patients than the all-comers strategy, against a population where the comparator anti-PD-1 has the lowest expected efficacy.

This is the inverse of how most upstream-mechanism trials are currently designed. The standard approach is to enrol PD-L1 positive patients on the assumption that they are the most "immune-engaged" and therefore most likely to respond to anything that boosts immunity. Encounter's read suggests the opposite for upstream agents: PD-L1 positive patients are the ones whose cycle has already committed, and an upstream drug has the least to add for them. The patients where an upstream drug should produce the largest absolute lift are the ones the field is currently excluding from upstream-drug trials. The screening burden is real — 3.45× the enrolment count — but the population is the population where the experiment actually answers the question.

For a trial with both a synapse-acting and an upstream-acting arm — increasingly common in combination Phase 2s — the strongest design is biomarker-stratified randomisation: enrol all-comers, stratify on the structural call, and analyse each arm against its matched control within the structural class. This recovers the benefit of structural selection without requiring a screening exclusion at enrolment, and produces two interpretable read-outs from a single trial.

What this changes about the IND-enabling package

For sponsors preparing an IND or a Phase 1b expansion cohort, the structural call is also a tool for defining the patient subpopulation in the protocol's hypothesis. Instead of writing "we hypothesise that high-PD-L1 patients will respond at X% to combination Y," the framework supports a more specific hypothesis: "we hypothesise that patients past Activation by structural classification will respond at X% to the synapse-acting arm and that patients held at Activation will respond at Y% to the upstream-acting arm." That hypothesis is testable in a single trial of moderate size and produces a dataset that can support a regulatory argument for a structural companion diagnostic in Phase 3.

Comparator landscape

The 26.8 percentage point separation produced by the structural call exceeds what published biomarkers achieve on overlapping or similar source cohorts. The comparison is approximate because each method has been evaluated on slightly different patient pools, with different cutoffs, and using different normalisation pipelines. The numbers below are drawn from the original publications and from independent validation reports against the four sub-cohorts that overlap with EXPRESSO.

Method Input Reported gap (responders vs non) Cohort overlap
PD-L1 IHC (≥1% TPS) Tissue staining ~8–12pp 4 of 6 sub-cohorts
TMB (≥10 mut/Mb) WES / panel sequencing ~10–14pp 3 of 6 sub-cohorts
T-cell-inflamed GEP (Ayers et al.) Bulk RNA, 18 genes ~14–18pp 5 of 6 sub-cohorts
IMPRES (Auslander et al.) Bulk RNA, 15 gene pairs ~16–22pp 4 of 6 sub-cohorts
EXPRESSO (Wang et al.) Bulk RNA, ML model ~18–24pp 6 of 6 (training set)
Encounter structural call Bulk RNA, structural panel 26.8pp 6 of 6 sub-cohorts

Three observations. First, the structural call uses fewer features than every other method on the list — a small panel chosen by the framework before any cohort fitting, against whole-transcriptome or 15+ gene-pair signatures used by the alternatives. The compactness is not coincidental: the framework predicts that a small number of orthogonal compartments carry the structural signal. Second, the gap is larger than methods that were trained directly on overlapping cohorts (EXPRESSO, IMPRES). Third, the structural call is not a competitor to PD-L1 or TMB — it answers a different question, and the patients it disagrees with PD-L1 about are the patients where the disagreement matters most for trial design.

An EXPRESSO-trained model and a structural classifier are not commensurable. EXPRESSO and IMPRES are optimised against the response label and will collapse if the next cohort has a different response distribution. The structural call is optimised against nothing — it is the framework's prediction about which compartment ratios indicate that the immune cycle has reached a particular position — and its output is a mechanistic statement that does not depend on the response label being available. This is the relevant property for prospective use: the call can be made before the trial reads out, with no expectation that a per-trial recalibration will be needed.

Methods

Cohort assembly

240 metastatic cutaneous melanoma patients with pre-treatment bulk tumour RNA-seq and confirmed RECIST response. Patients were drawn from six published trials harmonised under the EXPRESSO multi-cohort pipeline (Wang et al., 2021): GSE123728 (n=13), GSE160638 (n=28), GSE91061 / Riaz et al. 2017 (n=49), Gide et al. 2019 (n=41), Liu et al. 2019 (n=54), Puch-Solis et al. 2021 (n=55). All patients received anti-PD-1 monotherapy (pembrolizumab or nivolumab). Some had received prior anti-CTLA-4. RECIST response is reported as binary (CR/PR vs SD/PD) per the source publications.

The structural calculation

Each patient was assigned a single quantity, here called the structural score, computed from the log₂-normalised expression values of a small panel of bulk RNA-seq markers selected by the framework before any cohort fitting. The panel measures the balance between effector output and proliferation; the sign of the difference indicates whether the effector compartment exceeds the proliferating compartment at the bulk level. Patients with negative score were classified as held at Activation; patients with non-negative score were classified as past Activation. The cutoff at zero is structural, not empirical: it reflects the threshold at which effector output begins to exceed proliferation output, which the framework predicts as the binary commitment moment in the immune cycle. No cohort-specific tuning was performed.

Statistics

Wilson 95% confidence intervals for response rates were computed in pure Python using the standard score interval. The activation-vs-rest comparison used Pearson's χ² with 1 degree of freedom on a 2×2 contingency table; p-values were computed via the Wilson-Hilferty cube-root approximation to the normal distribution and verified against scipy.stats.chi2_contingency on a side computation. Two-proportion z-test sample sizes were computed using the standard formula at α=0.05, power=0.80. All source code is reproducible from the cohort JSON file shared on request.

What was not done

No multiple-testing correction is reported because only a single ex ante hypothesis was tested (structural score < 0 vs ≥ 0). No covariate adjustment for prior therapy, sex, age, or LDH was performed; the source cohorts heterogeneously report these covariates and a uniform adjustment would introduce more bias than it removes. Tumour purity correction was not applied — the structural argument is that purity differences are part of what the proliferation denominator captures, not a confound to be adjusted away.

Limitations and caveats

Retrospective. This is a retrospective analysis of cohorts assembled and analysed before the structural classifier was applied. The patients, the response data, and the gene expression measurements all existed at the time of analysis. The classifier was specified ex ante by the framework and applied without tuning, but the analysis is not a prospective prediction.

The flat sub-cohorts are real. Two of the six sub-cohorts (GSE91061 and liu19) show essentially no separation between the two structural classes. Plausible explanations are given in the Evidence section, but the framework predicts that the classification should work on every sub-cohort, and on these two it does not. A prospective trial would need to confirm that these are sample-specific noise effects rather than systematic failures of the calculation.

The cutoff is structural, not optimised. The cutoff at structural score = 0 is the framework's prediction of the commitment threshold, not the cutoff that maximises the response gap. Empirically, cutoffs near zero all produce gaps of 22–27 percentage points on this cohort, suggesting the result is not knife-edge sensitive. But the chosen cutoff is principled, not optimised, and prospective validation should use the same principled cutoff.

Bulk RNA-seq mixes drain and signal compartments. The structural read at single-cell resolution would distinguish exhausted, regulatory, effector, NK, and renewal populations independently. Bulk data collapses these into the structural panel and loses the within-compartment detail. The classification is correspondingly coarser than what scRNA cohorts (Bassez 2021, Zhang 2025) allow, and the gap on bulk should be read as a lower bound on the signal that single-cell stratification could recover.

Anti-PD-1 monotherapy only. The cohort is anti-PD-1 monotherapy. Generalising to combinations (anti-PD-1 + anti-CTLA-4, anti-PD-1 + chemo) would require either an analogous classification on a combination cohort or an explicit model of how the second agent shifts the relevant structural call. Neither is in this report.
